## Three working principles

These are not slogans I expect everyone to agree with. They are the rules I have set for my own work.

1. **Mathematics that cannot be understood by engineers should be thrown away.** If a piece of mathematics resists translation into a form a competent engineer can grasp, then either the mathematics is not yet finished, or it has wandered too far from the things that motivated it. Either way it does not deserve to be kept around in its current form.

2. **Mathematicians should do the hard work of making mathematical objects understandable to engineers.** This is the dual of the first principle. The translation work is not someone else's problem — it is part of doing mathematics well. Diagrams, examples, working code, and honest expository writing are not optional add-ons.

3. **Engineers change the world.** Mathematics changes the world only insofar as it eventually makes contact with people who build things. This is not a complaint about mathematics; it is a statement about what mathematics is *for*, in the medium-to-long run.

These three together are what this blog is trying to do — proofs and pictures and code, in roughly equal measure, on quantum walks, knot diagrams, category theory, and whatever else turns out to need them.
